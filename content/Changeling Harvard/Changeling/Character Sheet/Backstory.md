# Mira Vogel — Backstory & Biography

## Origins

Born January 1, 2000, in Seattle's Capitol Hill district, Mira Vogel grew up between two cultures that never quite merged. Her father, Thomas "Tom" Vogel, is Boston-born — a practical man who spent his twenties in international logistics and found himself in Berlin in the early 1990s, when the city was tearing itself apart and rebuilding simultaneously. Her mother, Katharina "Katia" Vogel née Klein, was born in 1975 in Leipzig, came of age watching the Wall fall, and crossed into the West armed with a translator's fluency and an amateur painter's eye for quiet beauty. They met in post-reunification Berlin, married, and eventually settled in Seattle.

Mira grew up speaking mostly English. Her German came in fragments — dinner-table phrases, her grandmother's letters, occasional visits — and she remained quietly self-conscious about the gap between her heritage and her fluency. The household was warm, intellectual, and a little eccentric. Her mother Katia, now that Mira is 25 (the same age Katia was when she became pregnant), has begun asking gentle but pointed questions about grandchildren. Mira receives these with grace and mild internal discomfort.

Signs of strangeness appeared early. At five, Mira sat on the living room carpet and filled a sheet of paper with intricate interlocking patterns — vines, symbols, asymmetrical symmetries — before she could have learned to draw that way. When asked what she'd made, she said: *"I don't know. I just see it that way."* Further moments followed: remarks about how the air "smells rounder," long silences spent watching things no one else could see. Her parents consulted child psychologists. The verdict was "advanced creativity and sensitivity." Nobody used the word Glamour.

She grew up bookish, curious, and slightly apart — beloved by certain teachers, interesting to classmates but never quite at ease among them. Her prom night captured something essential: she went with a kind classmate who genuinely liked her, danced, laughed, looked radiant — and when the moment arrived for a first kiss, found herself not afraid but simply *distant*, as if the frequency everyone else was tuned to wasn't quite hers. The magic everyone else seemed to feel wasn't there. She spent the rest of the evening gracious and quietly confused.

She graduated, entered Harvard's comparative mythology program, and for two years found her footing: a world of ideas that matched the shape of her inner life.

## Harvard and the Unraveling

Midway through her third year, something began to fray.

Sleep became fitful and strange. Visions bled into waking hours — not dramatic, not obviously supernatural, but persistent: impressions that didn't belong to her, faint sensory anomalies, a sense that the world had a second layer she could almost see. Her grades slipped. Advisors grew concerned. A physician suggested medication might help.

What nobody knew — what Mira certainly didn't know — was that she was approaching her Chrysalis, and that the interventions being proposed would have accelerated exactly what everyone was trying to prevent.

Count Edevan ap Eiluned, who moved through Harvard's world under the identity of Dr. Evan C. Albright, Professor Emeritus of Comparative Mythology, recognized what was happening. Unable to intervene openly, he worked through channels — letters of recommendation, quiet words with department heads — to arrange a year abroad at a small German university near the Black Forest. Her family, grateful for any structured solution, enthusiastically agreed.

She had also, in a museum gallery one afternoon with her favorite professor, caught her first glimpse of what she would later learn to call Banality: watching someone who had spent a life cultivating genuine insight become worn and distracted by the small indignities of managing restless students. She didn't have the word for it then. The lesson stayed.

---

## Chrysalis

Germany didn't ground her. It opened her.

Her grandparents' property lay in a quiet stretch of Black Forest countryside — old farmland, older trees, a barn no one had used in decades. One night, drawn by a compulsion she couldn't name, Mira walked out into the dark alone.

She found a creature there — something from German folklore given chimerical flesh, possibly a Schrat, possibly something without a name she'd encountered — that should have been threatening. Instead of danger, it triggered an explosion of Glamour she had no category for.

The eruption drew the attention of a local freehold within the hour.

They pulled her back, introduced her to what she was, and undertook her fostering: a year and a day of careful instruction in what it meant to be Sidhe, what it meant to be of House Eiluned, what the Dreaming was and how to move through it without losing herself. She was given her fae name: **Myrrha**.

During those months, fragments of something older surfaced through her Remembrance — impressions from previous incarnations. A sense of betrayal. Trust given to the wrong person. A friendship that ended badly and may have ended *her*. The details remain vague. She carries the emotional residue of a past she hasn't yet chosen to excavate.

Also in Germany: a brief, warm connection with a fellow student — kind, intelligent, seemingly interested — at exactly the moment when romance had started to feel genuinely possible for the first time. Her Chrysalis symptoms intensified and she withdrew without being able to explain herself. The connection faded before it became anything.

After her Chrysalis, this memory grew more complicated. Her Remembrance 2 gave her echoes of past-life romances she hadn't actually lived — feelings that haunt her heart as if they were her own memories and aren't quite. The German boy became a symbol of something she'll never cleanly reclaim: a mortal milestone she can no longer approach without the accumulated weight of other lives pressing in.

## Return to Boston

She came back a year later. Resumed her studies. Took on a TA position secured through Count Edevan, who had by this point revealed his fae identity to her and established himself formally as her mentor. He is exacting and sparing with warmth, but present and genuinely invested in his way. The Crimson Spire became the center of her fae life; her TA work became both cover and continuation of her scholarly mission.

Her thesis topic — the changeling motif as a metaphor for cultural displacement in German-American immigrant folklore — is academically genuine and privately ironic.

## Dreamers

**Freya Halvorsen** — Harvard freshman, Comparative Literature, 18. Glacier-blue eyes, too many tote bags full of borrowed books, burning bright and already on track for burnout. Mira recognized her at a guest seminar: brilliant, brittle, and in need of someone to tell her that wonder isn't weakness. Their relationship lives in the quiet margins — annotated books passed like messages, late office hours that become existential conversations. Freya doesn't know what Mira is. She writes poems about her and generates Glamour without realizing it.

**Erik Mahoney** — Late-20s MIT student, systems engineering, recovering from burnout. They met through a mutual acquaintance. He runs a D&D campaign and invited Mira to join; she accepted. Their friendship is quiet, a little absurd, and unexpectedly sustaining. He generates Glamour through the world-building he does when he's at his best.

## Appearance

**Mortal:** Relatively tall, with dirty blonde hair falling in loose curls — ash-toned, lightening toward the tips, with a natural wave that hints at complexity. Clear blue eyes, questioning in the way of someone perpetually half a breath from asking something important. Wears glasses. Dresses neatly in buttoned shirts, long skirts, or structured coats with a consistent accent of subtle occult or mythological iconography: an embroidered hem, a symbol-shaped brooch turned slightly wrong, a thin black choker alongside a necklace she doesn't take off. Posture and expression radiate warmth and attentiveness. Visual reference: Diane Kruger.

**Fae (Myrrha):** Luminous and elfin, she resembles a Galadriel not yet tempered by centuries — youthful but ancient-eyed. Her hair catches light like mist. Subtle constellations seem to move just beneath her skin. Her presence is regal but quiet, and gives the impression that she sees things others do not. She retains her glasses.

---

## Personality

Warm without being naive, curious without being reckless. She teaches with patience and genuine enthusiasm. She draws personal limits slowly and carefully, but holds them once drawn. She tends to see people at their potential rather than their actuality — which is both a strength and, occasionally, a vulnerability she is quietly working on.

Mannerisms: talks with her hands, loses track of time in archives, hums without realizing it, laughs softly at her own in-jokes.

Her emotional register runs older than her years — a consequence of the Remembrance, and of what she has seen. She is still learning what is hers and what is a ghost.
