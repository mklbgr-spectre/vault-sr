### 📚 **Historiography:**

Historiography isn’t history itself — it’s **the study of how history is written**.  
In practice, it means analyzing different interpretations, methods, and biases that historians bring when writing about the past.

🔎 Example:  
When Mira looks at medieval texts about “changelings,” historiography helps her **understand how those interpretations changed over time** (e.g., how Enlightenment scholars rationalized fairy lore vs. how Romantic poets idealized it).

👉 **Key takeaway:**  
It’s about examining historical narratives as constructions — not taking them at face value — and asking questions like: _“Why did this historian tell this story this way? What cultural context shaped their work?”_

### 🧭 **Anthropology:**

Anthropology is the **holistic study of humans**, focusing on cultures, societies, languages, and biological origins.

For Mira, it would mostly mean **cultural anthropology**:

- Studying how different communities construct belief systems, myths, rituals, etc.
    
- Looking at changeling folklore as part of human cultural expression across different societies.
    

👉 **Key distinction:**  
Where historiography focuses on _how history is written_, anthropology focuses on _how people live, behave, and organize their cultures_, both past and present.
