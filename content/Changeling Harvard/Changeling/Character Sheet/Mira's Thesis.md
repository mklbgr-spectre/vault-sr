> **“Between Two Worlds: The Child-Switching Motif as a Reflection of Cultural Displacement in German-American Folklore”**

**Abstract:**  
This dissertation examines how tales of child-switching (the changeling motif) operate as metaphors for cultural liminality and perceived alienation, particularly among German immigrant communities in the United States. It explores how these narratives reflect anxieties about identity, assimilation, and the loss or preservation of cultural heritage, charting their evolution from Old World folklore into American folk traditions.

🔔 **When was belief in changelings and child-switching most prevalent?**  
The motif of changelings as explanations for “strange” children is strongest in **early medieval through early modern Europe**:

- **Early Medieval Period (5th–10th centuries CE):** Oral folklore about fairies, spirits, and child-switching existed, but much of this was syncretic with pre-Christian pagan beliefs (like those of the Celts, Norse, and Germanic peoples).
    
- **High and Late Medieval Period (11th–15th centuries CE):** As Christianity spread, these folk beliefs coexisted with religious doctrine. The changeling motif was often reinterpreted through a Christian lens — changelings seen as “demonic replacements” rather than simply faerie substitutions.
    
- **Early Modern Period (16th–18th centuries):** This is arguably when belief in changelings peaked in Europe, even as proto-scientific ideas began to emerge. Communities still clung to older folk explanations to understand illness, disability, or difference.
    
- By the **19th century**, such beliefs began to decline sharply under the influence of Enlightenment rationalism and medicalization — but folklorists (like the Brothers Grimm) collected these fading traditions, cementing them in literary form.

🔹 **Origin of the word "changeling":**

- The term _changeling_ comes from Middle English _chaungeling_, which itself derives from _change_ + diminutive suffix _-ling_ (meaning “one who is changed” or “one who has been exchanged”).
    
- It first appears in English texts around the **16th century**, but the concept predates the word itself and was part of oral folklore across much of Northern Europe.
    

🔹 **Usage in historical context:**

- In historical usage, “changeling” referred specifically to a child who was believed to have been secretly exchanged by fairies, elves, or other supernatural beings for a human child.
    
- The belief was common in areas like Ireland, Scotland, Wales, Scandinavia, and Germany, and changeling accusations were sometimes tragically used to explain children with disabilities or differences that couldn’t be otherwise understood at the time.
    

🔹 **Other language parallels:**

- In German folklore, similar figures exist but were called by other names:
    
    - _Wechselbalg_ (literally “exchange child”)
        
    - _Kielkropf_ in Alpine regions
        
    - Scandinavians used terms like _bortbyting_ (Swedish/Norwegian).
        

👉 So **"changeling" isn’t a modern or cute invention for RPG purposes — it’s a genuine historical term** rooted in English folklore traditions and literary history.

This makes it a perfect term for Mira’s thesis as an American academic studying these motifs, because she’s working from **an Anglophone academic tradition** (hence “changeling motif”) even when examining German folklore (where _Wechselbalg_ would have been used originally).

🌸 **East Asia:**

- **China:**
    
    - Traditional Chinese folklore _doesn’t have a direct changeling equivalent_ (i.e., fairies swapping babies), but there are related beliefs about spirit interference with children’s health and behavior.
        
    - Some regional superstitions held that mischievous fox spirits (_huli jing_) or ghosts could possess children or harm them in the womb, but rather than “replacing” them, these spirits were blamed for illness or odd behavior.
        
- **Japan:**
    
    - Similar situation: _yōkai_ (spirits, monsters) like _kitsune_ (fox spirits) could **possess humans**, including children, but they weren’t thought to swap babies outright.
        
    - Japan has the motif of children who are "not right" (possibly due to possession or spirit influence), but not a changeling-style replacement myth.
        
- **Korea:**
    
    - The idea of spirit interference is strong in Korean shamanism (_muism_), with rituals designed to ward off malicious spirits harming children, but again — **not an exchange myth like the European changeling**.
        

---

🌙 **Middle East:**

- **Arab cultures:**
    
    - Beliefs around the **jinn (or djinn)** include the possibility that jinn might steal or harm infants, or even replace them with sickly creatures called _tabi’a_.
        
    - _Tabi’a_ refers to a demoness who could substitute a healthy child with a sickly one, which _very closely resembles the changeling idea_.
        
    - This belief is not identical but **functionally similar in that it explains sudden illness or difference in children through supernatural interference**.
        
- **Persian traditions:**
    
    - In Zoroastrian and later Islamic Persian folklore, spirits or demons (_divs_ or _jinn_) could harm children, but **replacement was less emphasized than possession or spiritual attack**.

🔎 **From a psychological and anthropological standpoint**, whether a community frames the explanation as a changeling swap, a fox-spirit possession, or jinn interference, **the core phenomenon is the same:**

- **Parents and communities trying to explain deviation from expected norms in children, especially disabilities, illness, or behavioral differences, using supernatural frameworks**.
    
- The motif fulfills emotional, social, and explanatory needs before medicine or psychology provided alternatives.
    

So for Mira’s thesis — even though **“changeling” is culturally specific to Northern Europe**, she could absolutely present these Middle Eastern and East Asian parallels as part of a **shared human pattern**:

> Cultures across the world externalized the fear of having a child who was _different_ by attributing it to supernatural interference — whether that meant fae, jinn, yōkai, or spirits. The details differ but the underlying anxiety about cultural belonging, purity, and the “wrongness” of certain differences is universal.
