🌸 **Expanded “German boyfriend” subplot:**  
Before her Chrysalis, Mira had never fully crossed the threshold of romance or physical intimacy. By her early twenties she was increasingly self-conscious about this fact — not because of societal pressure alone, but because she was quietly longing for a connection she couldn’t quite name, something deeper than just experience.

During her year in Germany, she met a fellow student: kind, intelligent, and seemingly interested. For the first time, romance — and the possibility of intimacy — felt within reach. Yet as their connection blossomed, so too did the symptoms of her impending Chrysalis: heightened sensitivity, disorientation, flashes of other lives intruding on her emotions.

Their brief romance never fully materialized, fading as Mira withdrew in confusion and distress, unable to articulate what was happening to her.

After her awakening as a changeling, this experience took on an even more bittersweet cast. Her **Remembrance 2** background meant she now carried faint but emotionally powerful echoes of **past romances** from previous incarnations — loves and heartbreaks she hadn’t actually lived, but which nonetheless haunted her heart as if she had.

The German boy thus became, in her mind, **both the one that got away and a symbol of an innocence she would never reclaim — a mortal milestone she will never “cleanly” pass through** because she is now forever touched by the Dreaming.

**Expanded Occult Club episode:**  
Mira’s natural curiosity and intellectual openness led her to attend a few meetings of what was styled as an “Occult Studies Society” — a secretive group that promised insights into forgotten knowledge, esoteric rites, and obscure mythologies. On the surface, it seemed harmless enough: candlelit discussions, symbol-drawing workshops, half-serious talk of ancient powers. But even in the early sessions, Mira felt uneasy; the club’s core members displayed a smug, exclusionary attitude, using occult aesthetics less for genuine understanding and more as a way to cultivate power over others.

Despite her unease, Mira remained polite and gave them the benefit of the doubt — until they invited her to their “true initiation.”

That night, in a dusty basement filled with mock-symbolism cribbed from disparate traditions, the society revealed its darker edge. The final test: a disturbing ritual that combined dangerous posturing with real harm — **participants were expected to draw blood with ritual knives and utter degrading oaths designed to cement loyalty.** There were whispered suggestions that the rite had other, more exploitative expectations if she passed this “first step.”

At that moment, Mira’s clarity broke through her curiosity. She realized how hollow their pseudo-spirituality truly was, how they twisted mystery into self-serving power plays. Quietly but firmly, she stood her ground, refused the rite, and left — walking away not just from that group, but from any future temptation to join circles that used the _appearance_ of wonder to dominate rather than inspire.

**Pivotal encounter with a favorite teacher and early awareness of Banality:**

> One formative memory from Mira’s early college years was a museum field trip led by her favorite professor, a renowned expert in mythology whose lectures had always seemed alive with hidden truths. That day, however, the professor seemed tired, distracted — worn down by the demands of managing the restless students rather than illuminating mythic symbols. It was a quiet moment of realization for Mira: even those with deep knowledge of wonder could succumb to mundane frustrations and let the “magic” slip away. She wouldn’t have known to call it “Banality” then, but this insight marked an early glimpse of the dulling forces that would later define her fae worldview.


### 🌸 1️⃣ **Prom Night that almost was**

Mira attended her high school prom — her dress carefully chosen, makeup painstakingly done, expectations swirling somewhere between excitement and quiet dread. Throughout high school she’d had admirers, but her warmth and nerdy curiosity always seemed to friendzone her in their eyes… except one classmate, a thoughtful but shy boy who _did_ ask her out formally.

They went together, but when the night arrived, Mira couldn’t shake a strange undercurrent — a vague but persistent _otherness_ that left her feeling slightly detached from the experience. She danced, laughed, posed for pictures… but when they found a private moment outside the gymnasium, when he leaned in expecting a first kiss, Mira froze.

Not in fear — but in _distance_.  
The magic everyone else seemed to feel just wasn’t there for her.

Her date misread the situation and gently backed off. He remained kind, but the spark was gone. Mira spent the rest of the evening with friends, gracious and radiant as ever, but privately confused why this rite of passage had felt hollow.

> 💡 **Why this matters:**  
> This moment reinforced a sense of emotional dissonance that Mira wouldn’t understand until her Chrysalis. She wasn’t “broken” — she simply wasn’t _human_ in quite the same way.

### 🌱 2️⃣ **Early childhood moment: “The crayons incident”**

At age 5, Mira’s parents discovered something uncanny during a mundane rainy afternoon. Mira sat quietly on the living room carpet, surrounded by a large box of crayons — but when they looked at her drawing, it wasn’t a typical child’s scribble.

Instead, she had reproduced an intricate pattern of intertwining vines, flowers, and symbols with near-perfect symmetry — reminiscent of medieval illuminated manuscripts.

When asked what it was, Mira simply said:  
_"I don’t know… I just see it that way."_

At first, her parents were delighted and praised her artistic talent. But as days passed, similar small moments accumulated:

- Her odd remarks about shadows “looking different today.”
    
- Her questions about “how the air smells rounder this morning.”
    
- Her tendency to lose herself staring at nothing, eyes wide, as if watching invisible shapes shift around her.
    

It sparked concern: testing followed, conversations with child psychologists.  
She was diagnosed with “advanced creativity and sensitivity,” but nothing clinical. Still, **this quietly cemented her reputation within the family as “the weird one,” albeit a beloved one.**

> 💡 **Why this matters:**  
> This incident could be Mira’s parents’ first clue that something about her perception of the world didn’t quite align with “normal.” It also lets you retroactively explain why she grew up self-conscious about being different, despite excelling academically and socially.

This small familial friction:

- Explains Mira’s **occasional insecurity about being “behind” in life’s milestones**
