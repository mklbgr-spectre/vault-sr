# Act 1 — The Grind

*Banality doing what it does: grinding, institutional, boring. Let it feel that way.*

---

## What This Act Does

- Establishes the space and the people in it before anything supernatural happens
- Gives the players a reason to care through their characters' relationships with Marla and the alley
- Introduces the developer threat as mundane and procedural — not a villain with a cape, a process
- Plants the two threads the party will pull on in Act 2: the inspection and the neighbors
- Ends with the players deciding to do something about it

The players have no idea the Glome exists yet. They shouldn't. This act is entirely mortal.

---

## The Space — What the Players Know

Before story pressure lands, establish how the alley works as a social space.

**The lanes and main floor** are relaxed. On a slow evening there are few enough mundanes that the kith who use this place can be relatively themselves — not a freehold, not formally warded, just a space where the Banality is low and Marla is behind the counter and nobody has to be careful.

**The back room** — behind the snack bar, past the pool tables — is theirs. No sign, no announcement. Everyone who uses this space knows. That's where you go when something needs to be said between fae, when a conversation requires the mundane world to stop existing for a moment.

**The Staff Only door** off the back room leads to the maintenance crawlways. Nobody goes there casually.

## Opening — The Free Scene

### Eröffnungsnarration *(read aloud to open the session)*

> Es gibt Orte, die sich anfühlen, als hätten sie die Zeit vergessen — nicht weil sie eingeschlafen wären, sondern weil sie sich entschieden haben, ihr eigenes Tempo zu gehen.
>
> Rainier Lanes ist so ein Ort.
>
> Das Gebäude steht auf der Grenze zwischen Capitol Hill und dem Central District, einundsechzig Jahre alt und nicht willens, sich davon beeindrucken zu lassen. Das Neonschild über dem Eingang ist das Original — das „R" flackert seit Jahren, und irgendwann hat irgendjemand aufgehört, es zu reparieren, weil es inzwischen einfach dazugehört. Gegenüber wirft ein neues Glas-und-Stahl-Gebäude blendend gleichmäßiges Licht auf die Straße. Dieser Kontrast ist der Block, wie er gerade ist.
>
> Drinnen: acht Bahnen, abgenutztes Holz, das trotzdem fest ist. Der Geruch von Bahnenöl und altem Holz und etwas darunter, das ihr nicht sofort benennen könnt. Die Geräusche — das Rollen der Kugeln, das Fallen der Pins, das leise Summen der Anzeigetafeln — bilden einen Klangteppich, der sich nach ein paar Minuten wie Stille anfühlt.
>
> Die Stammgäste kennen diesen Ort mit dem Körper. Welche Diele quietscht. Welche Bahn leicht nach links zieht. Welcher Hocker am Tresen wackelt. Das ist das Wissen, das entsteht, wenn man jahrelang immer wieder an denselben Ort zurückkommt.
>
> Hinter der Theke steht Marla. Sie ist seit ihrer Jugend hinter dieser Theke — sie kennt die Gesichter besser als die Namen, die Gewohnheiten besser als die Geschichten. Sie weiß, was ihr trinkt, bevor ihr euch gesetzt habt. Sie lächelt selten breit, aber wenn, dann meint sie es.
>
> Heute Abend ist es ruhig. Nicht leer — ruhig. Der Unterschied ist wichtig.

*Then, casually, not as a formal round — ask each player:*

The session opens with a slow evening. The players are here, as they often are. Nothing is supposed to happen tonight.

Give the table space to inhabit their characters before any story pressure arrives. Three questions — asked casually, not as a formal round — draw each character into the room:

1. **Where in the alley do you end up on a slow evening?**
2. **What does Marla put in front of you without asking?**
3. **Who here did you last see, and roughly when?**

These establish location, Marla's read on each character, and inter-character relationships without anyone having to describe themselves. Let this breathe. Twenty minutes of inhabited space is worth more than an hour of plot the players don't care about yet.

## The Letter

Behind the bar, pinned to the wall with a pushpin, is an envelope. Sealed. Official city letterhead visible on the front. Slightly crumpled from being handled and put back more than once.

Marla knows what's in it well enough to have an opinion about not opening it. She's been in this situation enough times now. She hasn't opened it — not a decision, a postponement, and she knows that too, which is why she keeps glancing at it and looking away.

Players watching people rather than bowling will notice the glance before anything is said. High Perception or Empathy catches it early. A Kenning-sensitive character might notice something else: a sealed letter from a Banality operation sitting in a Glamour-warm space carries a faint wrongness, like a draft from nowhere.

**How it gets opened** depends on the table:
- A player asks — Marla deflects once, then opens it herself
- A player takes it down — she doesn't stop them, which says something
- Marla opens it herself quietly, sets it face-down on the bar — someone asks what it says

The third is probably most Marla. She'd rather absorb it herself first.

**What's inside:** The second inspection notice. Seattle Department of Construction & Inspections. Electrical systems compliance review and emergency egress assessment. Filed by a requesting party — case number visible. Scheduled date: two and a half weeks out.

She's seen the first inspection report. It said nothing actionable. She filed it away and felt relief. She didn't read the appendix.

---

## The First Report

If the players ask — *do you have the first report?* — she pulls it from a folder under the bar. She's had it for months. Summary page: nothing actionable. She never read further than that.

The appendix, if anyone reads it, has this:

> *Electrical panel, maintenance area (rear of premises): non-standard configuration noted. Wiring and junction points do not conform to current NEC standards. Functioning within acceptable parameters under grandfathering provisions. Recommend review if modifications are planned.*

Not a violation. Not cited. Easy to miss on a first read. Kyle didn't miss it. The second inspection is specifically targeting electrical because he read this page and handed it to his contact at the city.

**The gut-punch:** She thought the first inspection was a win. It was the map he needed.

The case number on the second notice can be cross-referenced at city hall to confirm it was filed by a party with documented financial interest in the property — useful if the players want to challenge the inspection's good faith.

---

## The Noise Complaints

Once the letter is out, the rest comes out with it. Marla has a stack of certified notification letters — six or seven, all the same bureaucratic format. Complaint filed. Respond in writing within the window. Each response cost her a lawyer review. The complaints say things like *noise levels exceeded acceptable limits on the evening of [date]* — vague enough she can't challenge them directly, specific enough to accumulate on record.

She doesn't know who filed them. The city doesn't tell her.

Then, almost as an aside — the kind of idea someone has been sitting with and hasn't said out loud yet:

*"I keep thinking... if we just had an evening. Opened the parking lot, put out some food, let people come. Half the block doesn't even know what's actually here. But I can't be the one to organize it. Not right now."*

She doesn't ask for anything. She moves on. But it's sitting there.

Solomon is the thread on the complaints. He was approached by Momentum, refused, and told Marla immediately. He's been watching the same neighbors since. He doesn't know exactly who said yes, but he has a strong idea. If someone asks him the right question, he'll talk.

---

## The Pressure in Play

Let at least one piece of the campaign land concretely — not just described but experienced:

- The inspector from the first round stops by to drop off a supplementary notice in person — clipboard, professional, brief
- A regular mentions they got a visit from someone asking about parking and noise on the block
- Solomon comes in for his usual end-of-evening and mentions his own Momentum letter quietly to Marla, not realizing the players can hear

Different kith will respond differently. Let that happen before anything is decided. The Pooka will want to act immediately. The Satyr will be angry. The Sidhe will consider their position. The Sluagh will already be three steps ahead and not saying so yet.

---

## Marla in This Act

Warm but strained. She answers honestly about the letters, the inspections, the complaints. She doesn't volunteer the full scope. She doesn't mention the Glome. She doesn't mention her father unless directly asked, and even then keeps it brief.

She is not asking for help. She is not performing distress. She is managing, and the players can see the edges of how much that is costing her.

---

## Act End

The act ends when the players decide to do something about it. Someone says *we're not letting this happen* and the others agree. The decision is theirs, not prompted by a supernatural revelation.

They care about this place. That's enough to start.

---

## Tone Note

This act should feel heavy in a specific way — the weight of paperwork, legitimate-looking processes, things that are technically legal. Resist the urge to introduce mystery or foreshadowing too early. The mundanity is the point. The players should feel what Marla has been living with for months before the session started.
