# Act 2 — The Turn

*Things seem to be working. Then the party finds out what they're actually protecting.*

---

## What This Act Does

- Gives the players real traction — their actions push back against the pressure campaign
- Delivers the Glome reveal through Marla's choice to trust, not through player snooping
- Reframes the stakes: this isn't just a building, it's what lives in it
- Ends with apparent victory collapsing into something worse

Two threads run through this act in parallel. They don't have to resolve in order, but both need to close before the pivot.

---

## Thread One — The Grill Evening

Marla mentioned it in Act 1 almost as an aside. The party picks it up.

She can't front it — if she organizes it herself it looks desperate, and the neighbors who filed would see it as pressure rather than invitation. The party organizes it. Marla provides the space, the grill, the food with Solomon's help. Someone else's name on the evening.

Solomon is the key operational piece. He knows roughly which neighbors were approached by Momentum and said yes. He's been waiting for someone to ask. He'll handle introductions, work the neighbors he knows, and make sure the food side runs. If the party asks him directly what he knows about the complaints, he'll tell them — he's been sitting on it.

**The evening itself:** Parking lot, early evening, warm enough. Grill going. Music from inside spilling out. Marla visible but not running it. The neighbors who filed come because it's their block and there's free food and nobody made it a confrontation.

They spend an hour remembering that this place is theirs too. They withdraw their complaints. Not dramatically — they just stop filing, and eventually tell their lawyers to drop it. The proceedings collapse for lack of standing.

The Satyr and the Pooka are built for this scene. Let them run it.

**The crew shows up.** Midway through the evening, Demarco, Tomas, and Cody drift into the parking lot. Nobody invited them. They saw the crowd, they smelled the food, they want to poke at something. They're not there to wreck the event — they're there to be a presence, to test what happens. Demarco does the talking. Tomas stands behind him. Cody says something he shouldn't.

This is a social scene, not a combat scene. The goal is to get them to leave without the evening unraveling. Marla knows them by sight — *"those boys, they're from two streets over"* — and deliberately steps back to let the party handle it. She's watching to see how they operate.

**Options for the party:** Talk them down (Demarco responds to strength or a genuine better offer, not appeals to conscience), make the crowd uninteresting to disrupt (Veiled Eyes on the event itself, or just shifting the energy), or apply low-level social cantrips. Cody's Willpower 2 makes him easy to redirect if the party needs a lever. If someone offers them food sincerely, Demarco takes it — he's not above that, and it lets everyone save face.

**They leave.** Eventually. The evening recovers. But now the party knows their faces.

## Thread Two — The Panel

The party knows about the appendix note. They need to look at the panel before the inspection date. When they tell Marla this — *we need to go back there* — she goes quiet.

**Marla's decision:** This is the scene. She's been protecting the Glome alone for two years, longer in the sense that she's known about it since childhood. She didn't tell them because she didn't know if she could trust the whole group with something this fragile. Now she has to decide.

She decides. Not because she ran out of options — because she trusts them. That distinction matters, and she makes it quietly clear.

*"There's something I should have told you. There's something in this building. It's the reason the place is still standing."*

---

## The Glome Reveal

She takes them through the Staff Only door herself. Into the crawlways. The maintenance area behind lane 7.

The Glome doesn't hide from people Marla brings deliberately. It knows her. It has known her since she was a child. Whether it warms to the party happens here, in how they handle the next few minutes.

It doesn't speak. It communicates through the building — vibration, the movement of mechanical parts, the quality of the silence. It is not aggressive. It is watchful.

**The panel:** The players can see it immediately once they're back here. It works. Better than any licensed work — the Glome has maintained it for sixty years by instinct and feel. But the wiring is unrecognizable as standard. Wrong gauge materials. Junction points in places no electrician would place them. Connections that make complete sense if you understand how this building breathes and none at all on a clipboard.

Reeves will write it up. That's enough to give Momentum the violation they need.

---

## The Communication Challenge

The Glome kept it *functional.* The concept that functional and compliant are different things is genuinely foreign to it. It has made this building better than any code requires. It doesn't understand that its work is the problem — not because it failed, but because it succeeded in ways the inspection system has no category for.

This isn't hostility. It's confusion. Like being told that speaking clearly and correctly is wrong because you didn't use the approved dialect.

**The practical challenge:** The panel needs to look like standard licensed work before the inspection date. This means either:
- Getting a licensed electrician in to re-do the visible elements properly
- Convincing the Glome to redo its own work to match what code expects

If the party tries to bring in an electrician without working with the Glome first, the building will make that person feel unwelcome. Not violently — lights wrong, equipment shifting, a cold it doesn't intend. The electrician will want to leave. The Glome isn't trying to obstruct. It just doesn't understand why someone is dismantling things it made.

**Working with the Glome:** The party has to communicate what compliant looks like — and that means the Glome has to trust them enough to change something it has maintained for decades. This is a relationship, not a puzzle. Characters with high Kenning or strong Empathy have an edge in making that connection. What the Glome needs to understand isn't code — it's that the humans with clipboards have a different idea of right, and that idea matters right now even if the Glome's idea is better.

When it works — when the Glome understands and redoes its work in a way that passes — the panel looks like ordinary licensed electrical work. Boring. Correct. Nothing to cite.

## The Inspection — Day Of

Dan Reeves arrives on time. Mid-fifties, professional, clipboard and camera. With him: Marcus Chen, junior inspector, three months into the job. Earnest, asks more questions than Reeves does, actually interested in the building's history.

They work through the checklist. Lanes, exits, back corridor, the snack bar area. Reeves is thorough but not unreasonable. Marcus is the one who wants to see the maintenance area properly — he has questions about the crawlway egress labeling that Reeves would wave off but Marcus won't let go.

**The Staff Only door:** They will ask to see it. Marla unlocks it without hesitation this time — the panel is handled, the Glome knows what's happening, she's ready.

The maintenance area looks like a well-maintained 1961 building. The panel looks like ordinary licensed work. Marcus notices that the overall condition is remarkable for the age of the building and says so. Reeves notes it without comment and moves on.

**Cantrip pressure:** If the party decides to use powers to smooth the inspection, Marcus is the one it lands badly on. He's just doing his job. The Mists will handle what he sees, but Banality accumulates and the party knows it. This should feel like a choice with a cost, not a free shortcut.

**Outcome:** Reeves finds nothing actionable. Momentum loses this avenue.

---

## The Apparent Win

The official result came in a day or two ago. Marla didn't call anyone immediately — she sat on it until she could have the whole group together. That's how she operates. She pulls it out over drinks: city letterhead, dry bureaucratic language, *no actionable violations found at time of inspection.* The parking proceedings are already collapsing for lack of standing.

Let someone read it aloud if they want to. The contrast between that language and what's actually in this building is quietly funny.

The Glome is known to the party now. The alley is what it always was but the players understand it differently. Give this moment room. The alley feeling like itself. A round of something. The first time in weeks Marla looks like she's not carrying it alone.

---

## The Pivot

The evening winds down. Time skip: it's 11:30. The party filters out together. Narrate this for them — the cool air after a warm room, the quiet block, the night feeling like theirs for once.

Then take it away from the players. This next part is yours.

**Marla alone.** She moves through the alley doing what she does every night — lights off lane by lane, chairs up, grill covered. She's smiling. Maybe she says something to the building. A good night, a thank you, something that costs her nothing because nobody's watching. A creak, a flicker. The Glome in its way.

She steps out through the front entrance into the night air. Turns to lock the door. Slides the key in.

Hands grab her from behind and shove her back inside. She screams once — just once, sharp and short — before the door swings shut behind them.

**Back to the party.** They're half a block away, heading for the bus stop. One of them had to pee, ducked behind something, slowed everyone down. They hear it.

They run. The front door is still open.

*By the time the result filed, Kyle already knew. He's had a day to make the call and a night to act on it. The party has been celebrating in the building that's being hit tonight.*
