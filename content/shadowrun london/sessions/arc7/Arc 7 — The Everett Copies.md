## The Setup

Seattle's Magical Crimes Unit has been working an unidentified body case for six weeks. In that time, four more bodies have appeared — each one in the tree line off the highway corridor north of the city, each one bearing the face of the same man.

Faces smashed post-mortem. Fingerprints destroyed. DNA from each body returns corrupted results — close to human baseline but wrong in ways the lab can't explain, and crucially, matching *nothing* in any database.

Then, five weeks in, they found the original.

**Daniel Okafor** — a British-Nigerian data analyst working a two-year contract in Seattle — was flagged via a partial match on a residency document scan. He's been interviewed three times. He has an alibi for every body drop. He is visibly distressed by the whole situation and completely unable to explain it. He's clean.

The bodies keep appearing. Okafor keeps being alive. Seattle MCU is out of ideas and the magical dimension is beyond their normal competence. They've called in outside eyes — the Knights of the Chalice, as an informal favour between equivalent organisations.

Drake sends three: small footprint, foreign jurisdiction, find out what this is and report back.

## The Truth

A researcher named **Dr. Casimir Veth** has been running an unsanctioned magical cloning project for approximately eight months out of a rented industrial unit in Everett, twenty miles north of Seattle. His goal: produce a perfect copy of a living person — not just physical, but mnemonic. A duplicate that carries the original's memories and presents as indistinguishable under normal scrutiny, but remains receptive to instruction at a deep magical level.

He is not there yet. He doesn't know why.

Veth selected Okafor as his template — not for any personal reason, but because Okafor's aura presented an unusually clean magical baseline, making him ideal raw material. Okafor has never met Veth and has no idea he is being used.

Veth obtains what he needs through **astral harvesting** — a technique he developed himself, pulling an imprint of Okafor's aura and memory structure during periods of low astral activity (sleep). Okafor experiences occasional vivid dreams he can't account for and sometimes wakes feeling drained, but has attributed it to stress.

Each copy Veth produces is physically convincing but mnemonic fidelity degrades badly. The copies have Okafor's face and fragments of his memories but quickly become unstable — disoriented, distressed, unable to function. The DNA corruption is a known side effect of the imprint process that Veth has not yet solved. When a copy degrades past the point of usefulness, Veth terminates it and disposes of it in the tree line north of the city. The face destruction is practical: he knows the copies don't match Okafor's DNA, but he can't risk a visual ID leading back to him.

**The artifact — The Flesh Fragment:** Somewhere in Veth's lab is an object he acquired about a year ago through a private collector — he described it in his notes as *"an anomalous focus of unknown tradition, pre-Awakening origin suspected, significant amplification of biological imprint workings."* He has incorporated it into his process entirely. He believes his methodology is sound and the artifact is a powerful but inert tool he's learned to use. He is wrong on both counts.

The Flesh fragment is the reason his process works at all. Without it, his theory would have remained a theory. With it, he produces copies that are almost people — which is extraordinary, and which is also why they degrade the way they do. The artifact's magic is doing something to the copies that Veth's framework cannot account for and cannot fix. He has been blaming his own methodology for eight months. The flaw was never his.

> [!note] The Tragic Reading
> Veth is a genuine genius. His theoretical framework is real and mostly correct. He was simply working with forces older and stranger than he understood, and the gap between what he thought he was doing and what was actually happening has produced six dead copies and a project that will never reach its goal. He will never know this.

**The funding chain:** Veth's arrangement is with a Johnson who represents what Veth assumes is a small corporate interest. The Johnson probably believes this too. The actual principal is the **Patron** — who identified Veth's work, recognised the Flesh artifact as the thing making it viable, and has been funding the project not for the copies but to keep the artifact in active use. Once the artifact is primed — which, after eight months of continuous application, it almost certainly is — the Patron's interest in Veth's project ends.

The first body was messy — a copy that died badly, and Veth panicked. By the fourth and fifth, he is clinical. He has a procedure now.

> [!note] Veth's Moral Logic
> Veth does not think of the copies as people. They are failed iterations — technically alive, briefly, but without the continuity of self that makes personhood meaningful. He has constructed this position carefully and believes it. It is wrong. The copies are aware during their brief lives, and afraid. He knows this and has decided it doesn't count.

## Daniel Okafor

**The original.** British-Nigerian, late 30s, data analyst. Moved to Seattle on a two-year corporate contract. Thoughtful, dry sense of humour, currently operating at a low hum of anxiety that has become his new normal.

He has been interviewed by Seattle MCU three times. He has answered every question honestly. He has no idea what is happening to him and the experience of being repeatedly treated as a suspect in his own inexplicable situation has worn him down. By the time the party meets him he is cooperative but tired — he'll answer anything, he just doesn't expect it to help.

**What he can actually tell them:**
- He's been having unusually vivid dreams for about eight months — nothing coherent, just strong emotional impressions that don't belong to any memory he recognises
- He sometimes wakes exhausted after a full night's sleep
- About three months ago he started feeling occasionally watched, but never saw anyone

None of this will mean anything to him. To Relay in particular, the dream description will land differently — astral harvesting during sleep leaves a specific kind of residue.

**Where he is:** Still in Seattle, still going to work. MCU asked him not to leave the city. He's complying.

## Structure

### Scene 0 — Drake's Briefing & The Flight

**Location: Mortimer of London, The Village — the platform, or Drake's office**

The Knights' hacker division flagged it before the formal referral arrived. KE Seattle had an unusual case — five identical unidentified bodies, DNA anomalies, magical signature nobody could place — and a British national as the only lead. KE Seattle passed it to KE London as a courtesy. KE London passed it to the Knights. Drake had already read the file.

She keeps the briefing short.

*"KE Seattle has a problem. Five bodies, same face, no ID. Their forensics consultant says it's magical, their magical crimes detective says it's above her pay grade. They've got a British national in the middle of it who keeps coming up clean. That's the official reason you're going — consular interest, welfare check, all very proper."*

*"Unofficially — something is making copies of a living person and disposing of them in the woods outside Seattle. Find out what it is. If it needs containing, contain it. If it needs bringing back, bring it back. Don't create an incident."*

She slides three tickets across the desk. Commercial. A connection through Reykjavik.

*"Consider it a trip. Seattle's nice this time of year."*

> [!note] What Drake Doesn't Know
> Drake has no reason to connect this to the meta-plot. As far as she's concerned this is a weird case that happened to involve a British national. The artifact angle is entirely unknown to her — the party will find it themselves.

### Scene 1 — Arrival & Briefing

**Location: Seattle-Tacoma International Airport arrivals, then KE Seattle Forensic Division — formerly the 23rd Precinct**

Solís is waiting at arrivals. She's holding nothing — no sign, no tablet — just standing where she can see the exit, watching. She spots them before they spot her, which is habit.

Introductions are brief. She's already moving toward the car before they're done.

**In the car — the setup:**

Solís drives. Seattle at whatever hour they've landed — rain on the windshield, the corporate skyline in the distance, the particular flatness of a city that hasn't decided what it is yet after thirty years of megacorp restructuring. Different from London. Wider. Less vertical.

She talks while she drives. No file, no display — she has it memorised.

*"I've been working magical crimes for eight years. I know what I'm looking at and I can't explain it. That's why you're here."*

She gives them the shape of it in plain language: five bodies, same man's face, found in the tree line north of the city over six weeks. DNA comes back corrupted — close to human but wrong, nothing in any database. Faces destroyed post-mortem, fingerprints gone. No ID on any of them.

*"My forensics consultant — Veth, you'll meet him — says whoever did this was pulling something off a living person. An astral trace, a memory impression. And then somehow building bodies out of it. Five times. I've been doing this job for twenty years and I don't have a word for what that is."*

She pauses at a light.

*"Then we found the original. A British national — data analyst, two-year contract, completely clean. We've interviewed him three times. Nothing. The bodies keep appearing. He keeps being alive."*

She glances at them.

*"I called your people because I needed someone who knows what this is. KE London said you were the right call. I'm hoping they're right."*

> [!note] Solís's Framing of the Knights
> She thinks of them as magical Scotland Yard — investigators who do what she does, just on a bigger stage and with a wider remit. She doesn't know the specifics of the Knights of the Chalice and doesn't need to. They're the experts who got on a plane. That's enough.

> [!note] The Imprint Setup
> Solís has just given the players the concept of astral imprinting in plain language before Veth uses it technically. When he says "mnemonic imprint" in the briefing room, they'll have the framework. She doesn't need to explain further — Veth will own the vocabulary from here.

A four-storey concrete block in the south of the city, built in the 2020s when the Seattle PD still existed as a public institution. Knight Errant absorbed the building along with the contract — new signage on the exterior, new terminals at the desks, same linoleum floors and fluorescent lights that have been humming since before anyone currently working here was born. The KE branding feels applied rather than built-in. The building has its own atmosphere and doesn't particularly care about the rebranding.

The forensic suite is in the basement. Lower ceilings, cooler air, the faint antiseptic undertone that never fully leaves a room where bodies have been processed for thirty years. Solís uses a small briefing room adjoining the main lab — a wall-mounted display, a table with too many chairs, a window onto the lab floor that nobody ever opens the blinds on.

---

The party meets **Detective Yara Solís** here. She has the case files ready on the display: crime scene photography, lab reports, the DNA anomaly data, the timeline of all five bodies, the Okafor interview transcripts. She walks them through it efficiently. She does not editorialize. She has been over this enough times that she has run out of new things to say about it.

**Solís's opening line, or close to it:**
*"I've been running this for six weeks. I know what I'm looking at and I can't explain it. That's why you're here."*

Partway through the briefing she brings in **Dr. Casimir Veth** — her forensic consultant on the case, the man who processed all five bodies. He is precise, helpful, and has a complete explanation ready for every question. He will offer the fake-death commercial application as the most likely motive unprompted — *"identity displacement, staged death, the obvious market for something like this"* — and suggest the party look at corporate extraction specialists or high-end fixers with biological magic contacts. It is a plausible lead. It is the road he chose not to take.

He processed his own work five times in this room. He knows exactly where each body is stored two floors up.

> [!warning] GM Note — The Early Reveal
> Play Veth straight — cooperative, clinical, faintly world-weary in the way of someone who has seen too many bodies. He should be memorable but not suspicious. The payoff comes in Scene 4 when the party walks into his lab and recognises him.
>
> The "executed" progression lands differently once they know: he was messy early on because he was still learning. By the time he was briefing the Knights in this room, he had a procedure. He has been the most informed person in every conversation about this case since it began.

> [!note] Solís's Tone
> She's not hostile to the Knights but she's not deferential either. She's exhausted and professional. She will be genuinely useful if treated as a peer, prickly if talked down to.

#### Scene 1 — Q&A Reference

---

**"How long have you been on this case?"**

*Solís:* "Six weeks. Since the second body."

*Veth:* "I was brought in around the same time. Shortly after the first body appeared, actually — Solís flagged it as potentially magical and KE referred it to me."
*(The first body appeared because he made it. He was brought in to consult on his own work within days of disposing of it.)*

> [!note] If anyone does the maths
> The first body dropped. KE flagged it as unusual. Solís called Veth. Veth has been in every briefing, examined every body, and shaped every theory this investigation has produced since day one. Nobody has done the maths on this yet. If a player does it out loud, let the silence sit.

---

**"What does the astral signature look like? Can you identify a tradition?"**

*Veth:* "The imprint work is hermetic in structure — systematic, methodical, no spirit involvement. Whoever did this thinks in frameworks. The biological component is unusual; most hermetic practitioners don't work at this level of granularity. It reads as self-taught in that specific area, built on top of a formal foundation."
*(He is describing himself with complete accuracy.)*

*Solís:* Nods. "That's consistent across all five. Same hand every time."

---

**"Were the copies alive? Did they suffer?"**

*Veth:* A beat. "They were alive in the technical sense — metabolic function, neurological activity. Whether they experienced suffering in any meaningful way is a philosophical question I'm not qualified to answer."
*(He is qualified. He has chosen not to answer it.)*

*Solís:* Says nothing. She has asked this question before and didn't like the non-answer then either.

---

**"What were they, exactly — copies or something else?"**

*Veth:* "The closest analogue is a deep astral imprint given physical form. Not clones in the biological sense — the DNA divergence rules that out. Something closer to a... cast. Made from an impression of the original rather than the original's genetic material. The mnemonic component is what makes it remarkable. Whoever did this was attempting to reconstruct not just the body but the person."

---

**"Why Okafor? What made him the target?"**

*Veth:* "His aura. An exceptionally clean baseline — minimal astral interference, stable emotional register, no prior imprint contamination. From a technical standpoint he was ideal raw material. The selection was almost certainly opportunistic rather than personal — he happened to present the right conditions."
*(True. He spotted Okafor by accident from his flat window.)*

*Solís:* "We've been through his background six ways. No corporate connections, no criminal record, nothing that suggests he was targeted for who he is. Dr. Veth's read matches ours."

---

**"What's the motive? Who does this and why?"**

*Veth:* "The obvious application is identity displacement — a perfect copy that passes mundane and most magical scrutiny would be extraordinarily valuable for staged deaths, witness substitution, corporate extraction. There's a significant market for something like this among fixers working at the high end. I'd look at anyone with the means to commission bespoke biological magic and a reason to need someone to disappear very convincingly."
*(This is the red herring. He is describing a road he considered and rejected.)*

*Solís:* "That's been our working theory. We haven't found a buyer or a client. Either there isn't one yet, or the trail is very clean."

---

**"You said the technique is becoming more refined — how do you know that from five data points?"**

*Veth:* "The first body showed signs of acute systemic failure — the process that produced it was unstable, possibly rushed. The subsequent ones show progressive improvement in structural coherence and a reduction in the indicators of that kind of failure. It's a small sample but the trend is clear to anyone who knows what to look for."
*(Every word accurate. He is the trend.)*

> [!warning] If pushed further on this
> If a player keeps pulling on this thread — *"that's a very specific read, how exactly do you know what to look for"* — Veth will answer calmly: "Forensic thaumaturgy is a small field. I've seen experimental biological imprint work before, in Europe. This is consistent with that lineage." It's not a lie. Let it sit.

---

**"Has anyone assensed Okafor? What does his aura show?"**

*Veth:* "I'm not a strong enough assenser to give you a detailed read on a living subject. The bodies showed significant astral disruption consistent with the imprint process, but Okafor himself — you'd need to do that yourselves."
*(He knows exactly what they'll find. He is handing them the thread.)*

*Solís:* "That's actually why I wanted magic practitioners in the room. Every time I've sent a KE mage to look at Okafor he comes back with 'something's off but I can't place it.' I need someone better than that."

---

**"What happens to the copies when they degrade? How do they die?"**

*Veth:* "Mnemonic collapse followed by systemic failure. The cognitive structure destabilises first — disorientation, loss of coherence — then the physical substrate follows. It's not prolonged."
*(He does not say: I have watched this happen six times. I have a procedure now.)*

---

**"Is there anything you haven't told the detective that you'd tell us?"**

*Veth:* Looks at Solís briefly, then back. "No. Everything I know about these bodies is in my reports."
*(True.)*

> [!note] Assensing Veth in Scene 1
> If any party member assenses Veth during the briefing: he reads as a stressed, sleep-deprived hermetic mage with an unusual astral texture — the residue of months of heavy projection and intensive biological imprint work. Nothing that screams guilt. Something that a skilled reader might file away as *interesting* and only understand later. If a player asks about it, describe it as: "worn in a specific way, like someone who has been doing the same precise magical work repeatedly for a long time." Don't flag it as suspicious. Let them decide what to do with it.

### Scene 2 — Okafor
The party meets Daniel. He tells them everything he told MCU — plus, if Relay or Willow assenses him, the astral harvesting marks are visible to a trained eye. His aura shows repeated shallow intrusions — like scar tissue from something pulling at the same place over and over. He has no idea this is visible.

This is the pivot point. The party now knows:
- Someone has been accessing Okafor's aura remotely and repeatedly
- The bodies are copies — imperfect, short-lived, and being disposed of
- There is a researcher somewhere who has been doing this for months

> [!note] Does Okafor Find Out?
> At some point the party will have to decide what to tell him. He is being harvested in his sleep. He deserves to know. How they handle that — before or after they find Veth, or not at all — is a character moment worth leaving to the players.

### Scene 3 — The Trail

The party has three routes to the lab. They will most likely take route 1, but all three are valid and can be combined.

#### Route 1 — The Astral Trail *(most likely)*

Relay or Willow reads the harvesting scar on Okafor's aura and traces its directional origin: west-northwest, consistent distance, the building directly across the street. The scar has a signature — methodical, repeated, the same practitioner every time.

In the building across the street: **Veth's flat, fourth floor.** If they project or enter, they find a heavily worn astral projection path aimed directly at Okafor's window. The flat is sparse — books, a cleared ritual workspace, the marks of someone who sleeps here between sessions and not much else. His personal log is here, not hidden — he never expected anyone to look.

**What the log gives them:** Okafor referenced by initials. A harvest schedule. The shell company name — *Meridian Thaumaturgical Consulting* — on a rental receipt and a transfer record. No lab address directly.

> [!note] Veth's Aura — if assensed in Scene 1
> If anyone assensed Veth during the briefing, they can now contextualise what they saw: a stressed, sleep-deprived mage with an unusual astral texture from months of heavy projection and biological imprint work. Not a red flag on its own. In retrospect, unmistakable. This is a good moment to let the player who rolled it have their realisation beat.

---

#### Route 2 — The Dump Site Corridor *(fallback)*

Cross-referencing three or more body locations produces a geographic funnel pointing north toward Everett. Solís has this data but couldn't close it without magical support — the party can finish what she started by reading the residual astral signature off each site for directionality. Leads directly toward the lab's general area rather than Veth personally.

Combined with the shell company name from route 1, this narrows to a specific address.

---

#### Route 3 — Veth Slips *(least likely, party are mages not forensic scientists)*

If the party engages with the forensics in Scene 1 and pushes Veth on specifics, he's *too good*. He describes the perpetrator's methodology improving over time with a precision that implies more familiarity than a consultant should have. If pressed on how he knows the technique is "remarkably stable" from five data points, he has to work for the answer.

This route is unlikely given the party composition but worth having ready.

#### Closing the Gap — Finding the Lab Address

The flat gives them *Meridian Thaumaturgical Consulting* but not a street address. One more step required — each PC has a natural lane:

**Matrix trace (Willow or favour):** Shell company registration → mail forwarding service → commlink ID → triangulates to an Everett industrial zone. No decker in the party, so this means either calling in a Knights Cyber Favour or asking Solís to pull it through KE channels.

> [!warning] The Solís Complication
> Asking Solís to run the shell company means telling her that her trusted forensic consultant is the suspect. She will need a moment. She will then be very, very focused. How she handles Veth from that point — whether she wants to be present for the confrontation, whether she tries to pull him in through official channels first — is a player-facing decision worth leaving open.

**Physical legwork (Shane):** Veth drove bodies north repeatedly in a rented vehicle, always the same rental place, always cash, always early morning returns. The attendant remembers him — quiet, polite, distinctive. Vehicle rental records pulled through KE or legwork on the ground produce a partial description and a return-route data point that narrows the area.

**Astral tracking (Relay):** Months of carrying fresh biological constructs north along the same route leave a faint astral smear — not a clean trail, but a gradient. Requires a good roll and creative thinking. Reward it if they try: the trail points north and fades toward Everett, consistent with the other leads.

### Scene 4 — Veth's Lab *(The Setpiece)*

An industrial unit on the outskirts of Everett. From the outside: unremarkable, rented under a shell name, no signage, no obvious activity. A mage assensing the exterior before entry will feel something — not danger exactly, but *weight*. Something old and wrong pressed into the walls from months of use.

**The establishing image:** The party enters and sees the tank first. A large vessel, apparatus-fed, humming quietly. Inside: a mass of tissue in early formation — recognisably human in outline, not yet in detail. Flesh soup actively becoming something. The artifact is worked prominently into the feeding apparatus, glowing faintly with a light that has no mundane source. The rest of the lab is immaculate — hand-drawn aura diagrams covering the walls, six empty containment spaces with heavy astral warding, records filed with forensic precision.

Veth is at his workbench. He is not expecting visitors. He turns, looks at them, and is quiet for a moment.

Then: *"You're faster than I expected."*

#### The Conversation

Veth does not reach for the gun immediately. He is one man and he knows it. What he does first is talk — because the party is the first people who will actually understand what they're looking at, and eight months alone with this work has left him with things he needs to say.

He is not performing innocence. He knows why they're here. What he wants — the thing he'll never quite ask directly — is for someone to understand what the work actually *was* before it ends.

**What he'll tell them, unprompted or with minimal pressure:**
- The theory is real. Consciousness can be reconstructed from an astral imprint. He is certain of this. The copies fail not because the theory is wrong but because something in the integration process degrades — a problem he hasn't solved yet.
- He chose Okafor because his aura was exceptional. Not personal. He would have chosen anyone with that baseline.
- He knows what the copies experienced. He decided it didn't count. He will say this plainly, without flinching, because he has examined the decision carefully and stands by it. Whether he actually does is another matter.
- The control angle — if it comes up — he dismisses with mild contempt. *"That was a funding conversation. It was never the work."*

**What he won't say:**
- That he read the Month 6 log entry and chose not to follow the question
- That he sat across from Okafor and felt something he filed away
- Whether he thinks the work was worth it

> [!note] The Question Underneath
> Veth doesn't ask *"was it worth it?"* out loud. But it's there. The party may ask it for him. He won't have an answer. That's correct — he shouldn't. If a player pushes on this, let the silence sit. It's the most honest moment in the scene.

**The window closes when** the party makes clear they're not going to let him continue — not arresting him, not negotiating, just: *this ends tonight.* At that point he picks up the gun.

---

#### The Confrontation

Veth is not a combat mage. He is, however, a precise man with eight months of granular aura work behind him and a gun in his hand.

**His tactics:**
- Gun trained on the most physically present threat (Shane) as a deterrent while he works astrally
- Astrally, he targets Willow — unknown tradition, harder to read, higher threat to him than a physical adept
- He is surgical, not aggressive. He's trying to create space to escape or negotiate, not to win a fight.
- He has no contingency. He never seriously planned for being found. This arrogance is his actual weakness — he's improvising.

**The artifact:** It is worked into the apparatus and cannot be simply pocketed mid-fight. Someone has to disengage it deliberately — a deliberate action, taking their full attention for a moment, during which they are not doing anything else. Relay is the natural candidate given her tradition's resonance with what the artifact is.

When she touches it to disengage it, she should feel its age. Not the lab. Not Veth's work. Something far older — pre-Awakening, patient, indifferent to everything that has happened around it for the past eight months. It has been here the whole time. It will be somewhere else next.

*Describe it as:* an object that feels like it has been waiting. Not for her specifically. Just waiting.

Once disengaged the apparatus stops — the tank begins to go quiet. The artifact can then be bagged and transported. It looks unremarkable: a small carved stone disc, dark grey, with markings that don't match any tradition Relay knows. It is warm to the touch in a way that has nothing to do with the room temperature.

> [!note] Veth and the Artifact
> If Veth is still conscious when the artifact is removed, he will watch it go with an expression that is hard to read. He may say: *"I never understood what it was."* He won't elaborate. That's the most honest thing he says all session.

**Ending the scene:**
- Veth taken into custody: Knights will want him. Solís will want him. There will be a jurisdictional conversation. Both can have him eventually.
- Veth doesn't survive: also valid. He was pointing a gun at people.
- The apparatus: does someone shut it down? The tank keeps running unless someone stops it. The party has to actively decide. Nobody has to say anything about it — just: does someone reach over and turn it off?

> [!note] The Tank
> Don't editorialize about the tank. Don't tell them what shutting it down means or doesn't mean. Just note that it's still running and leave the space. The players know what's in it. They know what it would have become. That's enough.

#### Solís in Scene 4

She walks in with the party. She sees Veth. He sees her.

Half a second. Then he says *"You're faster than I expected"* and the scene begins.

**During the talk window:** Solís doesn't speak. She stands slightly behind the party and lets them lead. She is very still. Her face adept read is finally firing correctly — not deception, just the absence of guilt that should have been there in every briefing for six weeks. That absence is its own specific feeling. She is processing what it means that she sat across from him repeatedly and felt a tired, dedicated professional doing difficult work. Because he was. That was always true. It just wasn't the whole truth.

If a player looks at her during this phase: she's watching Veth. Not with anger. With the particular focus of someone recalibrating everything.

**When it turns physical:** Solís draws. She has jurisdiction the party doesn't — if Veth needs to be detained, she's the one who can do it legally. She won't fire unless she has to, but she will put herself between Veth and an exit. Twenty years on the job. This part she knows.

**After:**

When it's over — Veth in custody, or not — give her a moment. Not a breakdown. Just a moment where she stands in the lab and looks at the diagrams on the walls and the records filed with forensic precision and the tank still running, and doesn't say anything.

Then she'll be practical again. Evidence to log. Reports to file. A very uncomfortable conversation with her supervisor about her forensic consultant.

> [!note] What She Says, If Asked How She's Doing
> *"I've been doing this job for twenty years. I know what I'm looking at."*
>
> Same line as the car. Different weight now.

**The jurisdictional question — if Veth is alive:**
- Solís wants him processed through KE Seattle. He committed crimes in her jurisdiction.
- The Knights want him — or at minimum his knowledge of the artifact and the Johnson chain.
- Reasonable resolution: KE Seattle books him, Knights get access for debrief. Solís facilitates it because she owes them one and she knows it.
- If the party handled her well all session, she'll make it easy. If they were difficult, she'll make it slightly less easy. Either way she'll do it.

## Yara Solís — MCU Contact

Seattle Magical Crimes Unit detective. Mid-40s, Latina, twenty years on the job. **Low-level face adept** — her abilities are social rather than combat: heightened empathy, an instinctive read on emotional states, the kind of presence that makes interview subjects feel simultaneously comfortable and exposed. She is exceptionally good at knowing when someone is lying.

This case has rattled her, and she knows exactly why: her instincts have returned nothing on this. Six weeks, multiple interviews with Okafor, regular contact with Veth, and not a flicker of wrongness from either of them. She's starting to doubt herself, which is not a state she's used to.

She called for outside help, which cost her something professionally. She's aware of that too.

She will share everything. She wants this closed.

> [!note] Why Veth Beat Her
> Veth never lied to her. He answered every question she asked with complete accuracy. His forensic reports are impeccable.
